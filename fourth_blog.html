MULTI-LAYER FARMING PROCESS AND BENIFITS

India is a secular country, and one of the key things that makes us proud of it is how we can find harmony despite our differences. Agriculture is another factor that unites us, though! Did you know that there are primarily three forms of farming, each of which is subdivided into several subtypes? We shall learn more about multilayer farming in this essay.

What is Multilayer Farming?

Multilayer farming is an integrated agricultural strategy in which 4-5 distinct crop varieties are planted simultaneously on the same piece of ground and mature at various times and heights. It is a form of intercropping, which is becoming more popular right now. The Makrao village's multilayer vegetable farming is a great illustration of how to use soil and water resources wisely to make the most of few land resources.

The nutrient solution flows in a very thin film of about 1-2mm in height, hence the name nutrient film technique. The continuous flowing of nutrient film is very essential to keep the nutrient solution oxygenated.

What Could Be The Objectives

  • Multilayer farming is the maximum and effective use of vertical space
  • Optimum use of per unit area of land for production
  • Harvesting of crops on regular basis all around the yearace
  • Conservation of water along with increasing productivity

    • Some of the benefits of multi-layer farming include:

  • You can produce more food while using up less room thanks to it. When dealing with a small place, like a balcony or backyard garden, this is extremely beneficial.
  • By rotating different crops each year, multi-layer farming also helps to prevent soil depletion since you won't need to add amendments (like compost or manure) every time you plant a new crop.
  • Because multi-layer farming gives pests and illnesses numerous targets to choose from, there is less likelihood that they will all attack the same item at once, which benefits both your family's food and your pests and diseases.
  • If you have too many tomatoes on your hands in July but not enough carrots in September, you may plant a second layer of tomatoes in late August or early September so that they will mature extremely early. This is another way that multi-layer farming can be used to adjust crop outputs over time.
  • Crop yields will grow, which you'll like. In comparison to conventional methods, many crops may be grown simultaneously in a single field, improving overall yields by up to 30%. This is so that nutrients from the soil may be divided among the plants, each of which has its own root system.
  • decreased losses due to illnesses and pests. Plants grown in many layers are more resistant to pest attacks because there are fewer bugs available to attack each plant. For instance, tomatoes grown below radishes shield them from fungus illnesses like Verticillium wilt, while onions placed above carrots shield the latter from assaults by the carrot fly pest.
  • Organic farmers may boost their yields through multilayer farming, which enables them to generate more revenue from their property.
  • By growing various species next to one another, one can reduce the likelihood that the entire crop will fail in the case of a disease or insect infestation.

    What is the benifit of multi-layer farming?

    Multi-layer farming is an old method that is currently regaining popularity. Polyculture or "intercropping" are other names for it. It refers to simultaneously cultivating many crops on the same plot of land, sometimes with various crops growing in the soil below and sometimes with distinct crops growing on top (the canopy). Multi-layer farming has several advantages. They consist of:

    increased output

    Because their roots nurture one another and more plants are working together to generate more food, growing many crops together boosts yields.

    quicker growth

    When cultivated together, many crops develop more quickly, especially if they are planted close to one another and get frequent irrigation.

    less weeds

    When weeds are left to grow unrestrained by human influence, they tend to push out slower-growing plants because they compete with crops for water, nutrients, and sunshine (weeding). However, if you intersperse quickly growing plants with slower-growing ones, you will have fewer weeds overall.

    Fewer pests

    Planting a variety of species minimises pest issues overall by making it more difficult for pests to obtain their preferred feeds. Pests prefer some plant types over others.

    a rise in biodiversity

    Because diverse plant species are cultivated side by side in one area, multi-layer farming contributes to a greater biodiversity. This gives birds, bees, and other insects the perfect environment they need to participate in pollination and other ecological activities crucial to the maintenance of robust ecosystems. Multilayer farming keeps the income flowing. By cultivating several crops, farmers may remain financially viable throughout the year. Additionally, it hires a lot of employees all year round. Additionally, it assists in reducing the impacts of heavy rain, landslides, and soil erosion. Because different crops require various nutrients, the soil is replaced with these nutrients, increasing its fertility. This method improves the soil's ability to retain water. Multiple factors reduce the chance of crop failure.

    Steps to perform multi-layer farming

    Here are the steps that are taken to perform multi-layer farming:

    1. Crop selection

    The crops that are grown vary. Crops need to mature at different times and reach different heights.

    2. Field preparation and selection

    Square and rectangle terrain are considered to be good for multi-tier farming. Composting, FYM addition, and tillage are a few examples of necessary procedures. Tillage creates a good seedbed for planting.

    3. Seed selection and treatment

    It is decided to use premium crop seeds. The ideal seed has a high production potential and is clean, healthy, and robust. To guard against soil- or seed-borne diseases, seeds are cleaned.

    4. Irrigation

    Water is applied to crops artificially during irrigation. One of the most important elements in plant growth is irrigation. The conditions for transpiration and photosynthetic activity must be met. The nutrients that are contained in the soil are dissolved with water. In multi-layer farming, irrigation is very crucial. There is a enough amount of water available. It is frequently advised to use a reasonable amount of water. The ring basin method works well for irrigation.

    5. Addition of manures and fertilizers

    To guarantee that crops grow and develop correctly, manures and fertilisers are utilised. Nutrient requirements vary depending on the crop. The basal technique is hence the one that is most frequently applied. FYM, compost, and NPK are crucial for crop development.

    6. Weeding

    In the field, weeding is done to get rid of unwanted plants. Hand weeding, hoeing, and sickling are efficient weeding techniques in multi-layer farming. To get rid of weeds, use weedicides instead. To kill pests and insects, insecticides and pesticides are utilised.

    7. Harvesting and post-harvesting

    Crops' beneficial components are extracted and kept in a secure area.

    8. Packaging and marketing

    The materials are packaged and sold on the market for profit.

    Growing crops using many layers is sustainable. It entails cultivating crops on varied soil depths, enabling them to be collected at various points during the year. The goal of multilayer farming is to utilise resources as efficiently and intensively as possible. Maximum crop yield is stressed while minimising fertiliser management and infrastructure expenditures.

    Farmers may profit from multilayer farming in a variety of ways. Some of its advantages include giving crops nutrients, using less water, causing plants to develop more quickly, and safeguarding them from pests. Both small- and large-scale farming may be done with this approach.
